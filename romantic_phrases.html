Note: Arabic is one of those languages that complicate things unnecessarily by making everything "male" or "female". Many words in Arabic are slightly different depending on whether you are talking to a male or a female. Thus "I love you" above has two slightly different words, أُحِبُّكَ if you are talking to a male, and أُحِبُّكِ in you are talking to a female.
In contrast, Persian is completely gender neutral and does not distinguish between "he" or "she" at all.

Possessive pronouns

In both Arabic and Persian, the possessive pronouns (my/your/his/her/it's/our/their) are suffixed onto the end of a noun. For example, in both Arabic and Persian "اِسم" means "name" and it is pronounced as "ism" in Arabic and as "esm" in Persian. In Arabic the first person possessive pronoun (my) is "-i", therefore "ismi" means "my name". In Persian the first person possessive pronoun (my) is "-am", thus "esmam" means "my name". To say "his name" you would say "ismu" in Arabic or "esmesh" in Persian.

Object pronouns

In Arabic, the object pronouns (me/you/him/her/it/us/them) are also suffixed onto the end of a word, as in تَتَزَوَجِيني؟. The object pronouns and the possessive pronouns are exactly the same in Arabic, so adding an "-i"/"-ni" to the end of the word can mean both "me" and "my" depending on the context.
تَتَزَوَجِي؟ means "you marry?" and تَتَزَوَجِيني؟ means "you marry me?". Another example: أُحِبُّ means "I love", by adding "-ka" you get أُحِبُّكَ which means "I love you (male)", and by adding "-ki" you get أُحِبُّكِ which means "I love you (female)".

The short form of the Persian verb "am/are"

Persian has something similar to the Arabic object pronoun suffixes. The verb "am/are" has a short form that can also be suffixed onto the end of a noun. You can suffix both a possessive pronoun and the short form of "am/are" onto a noun as in عاشِقِتَم. The word عاشِق is an old loanword from Arabic, and it means "lover". In Persian, you would first add the second person possessive pronoun (your) "-et" to get عاشِقِت which means "your lover". You can then add the short form of the verb "I am", i.e. "-am" to get عاشِقِتَم which means "I am your lover".
